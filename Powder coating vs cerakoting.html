Powder coating VS Cerakoting

Both powder coating and Cerakote offer protective and aesthetic finishes.
But they have some key differences that might influence which one is better for your project.
Here's a breakdown:

Material:

  • Powder Coating is primarily used for metals.
  • Cerakote can be used on metals, plastics, and even some polymers.

Application:

  • Powder Coating requires a multi-step process involving sandblasting or chemical cleaning, applying a dry powder with an electrostatic gun, and then oven curing at high temperatures (around 400Â°F).
  • Cerakote is applied like a thin-film paint, often with a spray gun or airbrush. It can be air cured at room temperature or oven cured for enhanced durability.

Finish:

  • Powder Coating is generally thicker than Cerakote, resulting in a smoother, more chip-resistant finish with a wider variety of color and texture options.
  • Cerakote is good at creating a very thin, lightweight layer that preserves the original dimensions of the object. It has decent, though inferior scratch resistance to powder coating. And comes in a vast array of colors and finishes, including metallic, matte, and camouflage.

Properties:

  • Powder Coating is excellent for corrosion and chemical resistance, good electrical insulation. Not ideal for high-heat applications as the curing temperature can be detrimental to some materials.
  • Cerakote is superior to powder coating in terms of heat resistance, making it suitable for engine components and firearms. It also boasts exceptional UV resistance and flexibility.

Cost:

  • Generally speaking powder coating is less expensive than Cerakote due to simpler application and lower material cost.
  • Cerakote has a higher upfront cost due to specialized materials and sometimes requiring oven curing. However, its superior performance in specific applications can justify the price.

Conclusion:

  • Choose powder coating for a cost-effective, durable finish on metal objects that don't require high-heat tolerance.
  • Opt for Cerakote for a wider range of materials, superior heat resistance, and a thin, customizable finish.
